# High-Journal Expression

Use this reference when the user wants the writing to sound more like a strong journal manuscript rather than an AI answer, translation artifact, or over-explained coaching note.

This reference adapts "talk-normal" style constraints to manuscript polishing. The goal is not informality. The goal is directness, proportion, and mature control.

## Core principle

State the claim cleanly, then add only the context that earns its place.

High-level journal prose usually sounds strong because it is:

- direct
- information-dense
- restrained
- structurally clean
- free of performative explanation

## What to remove

### 1. Throat-clearing

Delete openings that announce the sentence instead of delivering it.

Flag:

- "It is important to note that"
- "It should be emphasized that"
- "值得注意的是"
- "需要指出的是"
- "本质上来说"

Start with the actual claim.

### 2. AI-style framing wrappers

Delete meta phrases that sound like the writer is narrating the explanation process.

Flag:

- "In other words"
- "This means that"
- "Put simply"
- "简单来说"
- "换句话说"

If the sentence is clear, say it once and stop.

Do not add a second "plain language" sentence after the real sentence unless the genre explicitly requires it.

### 3. Negation-based contrastive phrasing

Avoid building the main claim by first rejecting an alternative.

Flag:

- "not X, but Y"
- "不是 X，而是 Y"
- "X rather than Y" when the rejected half is only scaffolding

Prefer a direct positive statement:

- weak: "This is not simply a correlation but a robust association"
- stronger: "This is a robust association"

Exception: keep the contrast when the scientific distinction itself is the point and both sides carry information.

### 4. Summary-stamp closings

Do not label the ending as a summary before giving it.

Flag:

- "In summary"
- "Taken together"
- "总之"
- "一句话总结"

If the paragraph needs a final claim, just write the final claim.

### 5. Decorative structure

Do not add bullets, numbered lists, or stage labels unless the content is genuinely parallel or sequential.

High-level journal prose prefers paragraph logic over presentation scaffolding.

## What to prefer

### 1. Lead with the answer

Open the sentence or paragraph with the main result, interpretation, or limitation.

### 2. Positive declarative sentences

Prefer direct positive claims over rejected alternatives, especially in abstracts, titles, results highlights, and discussion topic sentences.

### 3. One move per sentence

Each sentence should primarily do one job:

- state a result
- interpret a result
- define a limitation
- position significance

Do not stack all four unless the sentence truly needs the density.

### 4. Controlled emphasis

Use emphasis through placement and syntax, not hype.

Prefer:

- short sentence after a dense sentence
- final-position emphasis
- tighter verb choice

Avoid:

- repeated intensifiers
- slogan-like significance claims
- inflated adverbs

### 5. Clean endings

End sections on a claim, implication, or next constraint.

Do not end with conversational offers, summary labels, or explanation-about-the-explanation.

If a concrete recommendation or next step belongs at the end, state it directly instead of staging it as an optional menu.

## Section-specific guidance

### Abstract

- open with the problem or finding, not scene-setting
- compress methods to only what changes interpretation
- let the last sentence land on meaning, scope, or consequence

### Results

- lead each paragraph with the result
- put mechanistic interpretation after the observation, not before it
- avoid explaining obvious transitions

### Discussion

- state the interpretation directly
- separate what the data support from what they suggest
- let limitation language sound controlled, not apologetic

### Response letters and revision notes

- answer the point directly
- remove defensive preambles
- state the change before the justification

## Boundary

- Do not remove scientific nuance in the name of bluntness.
- Do not force direct-positive phrasing when the scientific point genuinely depends on a contrast, exclusion, or boundary condition.
- Do not make the prose shorter by deleting evidence-bearing detail.
